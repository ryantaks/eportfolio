Looking at these two pieces side by side, the growth is pretty clear to me. The annotated bibliography came first — it was mostly about getting comfortable with the sources and understanding what each one was actually saying. It's solid work, but it's descriptive. The email is where things got more interesting. I had to take that same research and make it mean something to a real reader with real authority. Project 1: Beyond Irresponsibility — Understanding the Complexity of College Student Financial Behavior

February 13, 2026

This paper started with a question I actually found interesting: why do college students keep making bad financial decisions even when they know better? The research made it clear pretty quickly that the answer isn't laziness — it's a lot more complicated than that. Going through peer-reviewed studies for the first time was a different experience than I expected. The sources didn't always agree with each other, and learning how to work with that tension instead of just ignoring it was probably the most useful thing I got out of this project. The annotated bibliography pushed me to actually understand each source rather than just summarize it, which made writing the paper itself a lot more straightforward.

The common perception of college-age individuals is to view them as bad with money. However, when we examine this issue through peer-reviewed research, it loses a lot of its appeal. College student financial troubles occur for many reasons other than laziness or immaturity; there are objective gaps in the student's financial literacy, psychological influences on the student's behaviour, and differences between objective and subjective knowledge about finances.

Reflection

Reflecting on ENC 2135

Honestly, I came into this class not really knowing what to expect. I figured it would be another writing course covering things I'd already been taught. It wasn't that. The way ENC 2135 frames writing — through genre, context, and audience — actually shifted how I think about it, more than I anticipated going in.

The biggest thing for me was realizing that how you write matters just as much as what you're saying. Before this class, I treated most writing assignments pretty much the same regardless of what they were asking for. Now I actually think about who's reading, what they need from the piece, and what format makes sense for that situation. That sounds obvious in hindsight, but it wasn't something I was doing consciously before.

Project 1 was where most of that clicked. I picked college student financial behavior as my topic because it felt relevant — I'm a college student dealing with the same pressures the research describes. I went in expecting the paper to basically confirm that students make bad choices, and came out with a much more nuanced take. The research genuinely surprised me, and I think that ended up coming through in the writing.

The annotated bibliography was new territory. I'd written bibliographies before but never one where I had to explain why a source mattered and how it connected to everything else. It forced me to read carefully instead of just skimming for quotes, which honestly made the paper a lot easier to write once I got to it.

Writing the email to FSU administration was a different kind of challenge. Taking academic research and making it readable — and persuasive — for a real administrator meant stripping out a lot of the framing I'd built up over weeks of being deep in the sources. Getting to the point without losing the substance was harder than it sounds.

Overall, this was one of the more genuinely useful courses I've taken. I leave it thinking about writing differently, and I think that's exactly what it was supposed to do.
